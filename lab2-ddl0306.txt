In this practical, we are going to have some fun (yes, I do insist) discovering how to extract
the embeddings computed by BERT and to identify some of their properties, often described,
somewhat pompously, as their geometry. The practical is organised into three main parts : an
investigation of embedding anisotropy, a comparison of the representations of words belonging
to the same morphological family, and a comparison of the representations of semantically related words (synonyms, antonyms, and the like). It is preceded by a liminary fourth step devoted
to the constitution of the necessary resources, because embeddings, regrettably, do not generate
themselves. In order to maintain at least the illusion of a controlled experiment, we will also rely
on static word embeddings as a baseline.
The aim is twofold : first, to learn how to handle some essential tools (notably the Hugging
Face hugging-face library), and second, to explore genuinely interesting questions (at least from
my point of view !) without requiring unreasonable computational resources.

The goal of the first part of the practical is to compare
the embeddings of words belonging to the same morphological family, and to assess whether
(and how) morphological relatedness is encoded in static embeddings (GloVe) and contextual
embeddings (BERT). This necessarily presupposes two things : first, that we actually have access
to well-defined morphological families, and second, that each form in a family occurs often
enough in natural contexts to allow for meaningful comparisons.

considerably more fun, and extremely instructive).

representations. The underlying idea is that, by considering words sampled at random, we can
probe the structure of the representation space as a whole, rather than focusing on a small, handpicked subset of lexical items.

3 Morphological encoding
We now turn to the encoding of morphological information. In this section, we examine how
morphologically related forms are organised in the representation space. Rather than considering
randomly selected word pairs, as in the previous section, the central idea here is to compare
the distances between forms belonging to the same morphological family with those observed
between forms drawn from different families.
As before, we will start by defining a set of metrics to characterise these distances, before you
cheerfully move on to the experiments proper.
